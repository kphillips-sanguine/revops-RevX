Overview of qualification
Sales Qualification
Sales qualification is the process of evaluating leads/companies to determine their likelihood
to make a purchase.
Qualification is a very important part of our Sales Process to determine whether or not an
account, a lead/contact or an opportunity is going to buy from us or not.
We want to focus on the accounts, leads and opportunities that will drive the most revenue
and have a strong chance of closing.
Poor qualification leads to lower win-rates and wasted time for both you and the
researcher.

Building a Qualified Pipeline
There is a difference between a FULL pipeline and a BLOATED pipeline
• FULL = Quality and Qualified
• BLOATED = Congested with unqualified opportunities with a low likelihood of
conversion
Sanguine strives to only have FULL pipelines. In order to put an opportunity into our CRM,
it should be:
• Feasible (IC approved for unknown conditions/criteria, no concerns with criteria)
• Qualified for BANT
• General understanding of the pain points we are trying to solve
Let's dive down into these steps to properly qualify.

Budget Qualification
Budget Qualification:
The key is to get them to share their budget or budget expectations first. Always REVERSE
REVERSE REVERSE!
If someone has a budget of $500 a sample, what do you think that could indicate? It could
be $500 per mL. It could be that they are looking for inventory. It could be that they are
used to buying from the guy who sells blood from his garage down the street. All of this
means is you need to better understand and better qualify what their budget means, if
they're willing to go outside it (if yes, what value would cause them to want to go outside it)
and what is that process to purchase.
When you're dealing with a new lead especially, it's very important to find our how are they
typically procuring samples - so you can gauge what they are expecting, what their pain
points are - so you know if they will find value in our services.
Before you begin the budget conversation, you want to recap the prospect’s pain
points, desires, reasons for wanting to do prospective collection, frustrations, and
any other pertinent information that would aid in your ability to demonstrate
Sanguine’s value-add.
Sometimes people have trouble talking about budget and money because talking about
money can be awkward, but it doesn't have to be. Money is the language of business, so
it’s a conversation that should not be avoided. If you are confident in your pricing, if you
can prove your value, that will come across positively to your clients.

Budget Ballpark
Being able to ballpark accurately is very important.
If you ballpark too high, you may scare them off.
If you ballpark too low, then come back higher, your client may feel misled or may
not have the appropriate budget.
So how do you be successful with this exercise? Try to get them to share first.
If their budget is in the ballpark, then proceed.
TIPS
• Get really familiar with the Ballpark Calculator
• Know the Price books and price sheets
◦What might cause extra fees? custom protocols?
• Look up the tier for the condition the client is referencing
◦Know that criteria can bump it up a tier
• Give RANGES!
◦Ballparking is not a perfect science, but it will help gauge the clients reaction and
figure out where you need to be

Budget Objection
Budget Objection Handling:
If someone says:
“Your price is too high” or “Your price is higher than I thought.” That is a statement, not a
question. In other words, instead of trying to justify or defend our pricing, you can ask a
question like:
“When you say it’s higher than you thought, can you help me understand what you were
comparing it to?” or “Can I walk you through the pricing so you know exactly what it
includes to see if we are comparing apples to oranges?"
As you walk through the pricing, you want to make sure you tie it back to their pain points,
goals, desires, and criteria.
If their budget is not in the ballpark, here are some tools you can use:
• Pilot study as Monkey’s Paw
• Pivot to inventory or LeukoLots
• Fewer donors
• Broadening I/E

Authority Qualification
Finding out if you are working with the decision maker can be very tricky. No one wants to
feel undermined or reminded they can't make decisions, so it is a very delicate balance to
loop them in.
Things to consider:
• Look at the company and where they are located- each company has different
guidelines and gatekeepers, different cultures etc
◦For example: Novartis - Switzerland focused wouldn't want someone going above
their heads
There is a difference between a Decision Maker and an Influencer: Someone
may not be able to make the final decision budget wise, but they may have
strong influence in who they choose.
When you try to figure out their decision making process, you can identify who else may
need to be involved. There are things that important to ask:
• New clients:
◦Do we need a vendor agreement and/or MSA to work together?
◦If everything looks good from a proposal perspective, what would the next steps
be to move forward?
▪These vague hypothetical questions will get the client to walk you through the
process and then you can ask follow up questions

◦When they say they need to regroup with their team or send up for higher

Timeline Qualification
Timeline
If we aren't able to meet their timeline, the deal is likely not going to move forward. With
our prospective business model, it is really important to be able to give accurate timeline
ballparks to see if we can fulfill their needs.
Required
When a client asks you how long getting those samples will take, what do
you do:
•
Reverse and ask if there is a certain timeline they need them by.
- It's better to understand when they need them by before we give our response.
Give them the estimated number of months to complete.
- If we go this route, we are missing the opportunity to customize our response based
on their needs
Since we have longer timelines to fulfill, we really want to understand the clients goals and
timelines because this can also drive getting the study started.
It is always important to work backwards. For example, if it's January and they need all of
the samples by August (8 months away) and we are anticipating 7 months to collect all of
them, you can use this to drive your contract to a close by end of February at the latest.

Timeline Expectations
How long it will take us to collect all the samples depends on a few different factors:
1. Condition
2. I/E Criteria and how it affects prevalence
3. If an NPO is involved
Condition
*MR Retrieval should speed up once our new software is up and running
1. High proficiency populations (Tier 0 and 1)
a. Requiring medical records: Up to 20 patients per month + 2 months for Medical
Records Retrieval + SSU (ex. 20 patients = 3 months)
2. Moderate proficiency populations (Tier 2 - 5): Up to 10 patients per month + 2
months Medical Records Retrieval
3. Limited proficiency populations (Tier 6 - 7): Average 5 patients per month + 2 months
Medical Records Retrieval
4. Special Circumstances
a. Healthy Pediatric (Tier 2) Average 5 patients per month + 1 month SSU
b. NPO Required- 2 month study startup + 2 months Medical Records Retrieval
