Sales Process Overview
Sales Process
A sales process is a combination of stages and steps that help you guide the prospect from
“interested” to closed.
While we understand that each conversation you have with a prospect is unique, there’s no
need to recreate the wheel each time. The Sanguine Sales Process outlines the key steps
for you to follow so you can:
• Ramp up quickly.
• Replicate success and identify where you need help.
• Easily manage multiple deals at different stages of the process.
This section is built on our work with our prior consultant, L'areal Lipkins.
Implementing a Sales Process
1. Stages: The large milestones a deal goes through from discovery to close
2. Steps: The specific things that have to happen within each individual stage
3. Scripts: Talk tracks you use throughout the entire sales process
4. Skills: The skills you need to use the scripts and make the adjustment in real time
Let’s take a look at a detailed breakdown of the Sanguine Sales Process.

Processes Scale
Processes Scale
You may hear from your top performing AE, "I don't follow a sales process" but then every
single SDR who works with him/her can quote their go-to phrases, questions and client
stories.
It's important to have your process to provide you clarity so you know what you need to get
out of each and every call to drive it forward.
The more you follow your sales process, the more confident and competent you will
become. This will allow you to anticipate and address potential roadblocks with your
questions and talk tracks.

Mistakes
Common Mistakes in the Sales Process
There are several mistakes you can make in building your sales process. Let's take a look
at the most common ones:
• Not personalizing the process
• Skipping Steps
• Not understanding the prospect
Let's walk through these together.

---

<!-- Page 10 -->
Personalization
#1 Not personalizing the process
It is great to have a process so you know what deal gaps you may have, and you know if
the client is skipping ahead or missing something.
Each sales person is unique and they have different styles and this is a GOOD THING.
There is a reason we still have sales people, and not robots selling. People buy from people
they like. People want to connect with their sales person. If everyone did something the
exact same way, it would not feel natural. We can have the same scripts and do them
completely different.
I have trained many people, and one of the things I've noticed is the ones who believe
everything needs to go a specific way, try to check off each step in the "process" are the
ones who actually have the hardest time following their process. Why is this? Likely
because:
• they aren't guiding the conversation the way it needs to go
• they aren't actively listening to the client and diving down deeper
• they find it harder to pivot when the conversation doesn't go as planned (which it never
does!)
By personalizing your process, it feels more fluid, less scripted and it allows you to be
flexible enough to adapt to situations in real time without missing any steps.
When you master your sales process, it gives you more flexibility."

Skipping Steps
#2 Skipping Steps
Everyone wants to speed up their sales cycle, especially at the end of the quarter or end of
the year, but if you miss any crucial elements in your deal, it's likely going to slow your deal
down or cost you to lose the deal.
Sales people come to me and they say, how can I drive this to a close for this quarter?
Let's look at a few scenarios of questions I would ask:
• What is the driving reason for choosing Sanguine? If they don't know the answer, they
missed something in discovery or in that pain/PIE steps.
• When do they need the samples by? Do they even need them by a certain date? If they
don't know the answer, they missed something in the timeline step.
• What is their signature/PO process? Even if Sales knows this answer, it's important to
know how long each step takes, and if that aligns with the timeline you have until the
end of quarter. This is a true gap in the Decision Making Process.
Skipping a step leads to a deal gap, which in the end, can make or break the deal. By being
able to redirect the lead back where you need them to be, you are maintaining control of
the call and getting the information you need to ultimately drive it forward. For example,
when a lead asks for pricing before you even figured out what their pain is or their specific
needs are, you will need to redirect back to that discovery step.

Understanding the Prospect
#3 Not understanding the Prospect
This mistake shows the real importance of the discovery and pain steps. If you miss
something that is important to them, you may not be able to provide the solutions they
need and lose this in the end.
Do not skip the discovery step to better understand their needs. This is the key piece to
driving your deals to a close. It's also vital that you always know where a prospect is at any
given point in time and what you need to move them to the next step. This allows you to
track where you are getting stuck and the chance to get the opportunity moving again.
