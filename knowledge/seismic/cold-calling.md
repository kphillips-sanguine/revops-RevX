Overview
Cold calling isn't everyone's favorite. The fun phrase, smile and dial, may not leave you
smiling, but the increase in your meeting numbers will.
Email reply rate increases by 1.9% with cold calls
Why is that? The more touch points there are, it calls more attention to you. It brings more
name recognition and you're more likely to get a response, even if they don't answer your
call.

Openers
First 60 seconds is the most important. It determines whether or not:
1. Someone hangs up on you
2. They say to call back and block you aka never answer again
3. If they will genuinely hear you out and MAYBE schedule a follow up
So how do you make sure it's option 3?
First of all, don't start with these two openers:
Is it a bad time? or How's your day? This is just wasting their time, you sound like a

---

<!-- Page 4 -->
telemarketer, and you know what? It is a bad time because you're interrupting their day.
Instead try this:
• Lead with Context
◦Hi Bill, I work with many researchers at AstraZeneca...
◦Hi Joe, I work with some of your colleagues at Merck...
◦Hi Shiela, I work with a lot of CGT companies in the area...
◦Hi Jessica, we work with other *insert title* in San Diego...
• Intro yourself
◦I'm Chelsea at Sanguine
◦This is Chelsea at Sanguine
• Have you heard the name tossed around? *Say it with confidence*
◦Another alternative: Have you heard of us from your colleagues?
TIP
If you are calling a direct line, don't ask, "Is this Nick?," lead with Hi Nick - this shows more
confidence, you don't sound like a telemarketer/stranger and it catches them slightly off guard
(who is this? they know me? aka more likely to keep listening)
If they are the wrong person, they will correct you, and then maybe you have a new lead.
It's okay to stutter - that's better than sounding robotic! You are genuine!

What if they say No?
Truthfully, they probably will say no. Then you can make light of the situation:
• Guess I'm not as popular as I thought! Well the reason for my call is...
• Oh! Well we work with a few other researchers in your office and the reason I called
YOU is...
• Oh, I would have thought we would've crossed paths. Well the reason for my call is.
What if they say Yes?
• Oh good! What did you hear?
• Oh great, I'm surprised we haven't worked together yet. What do you know about
Sanguine?
◦Nothing just heard the name.... then you'll send up giving the same pitch if they
said no :)

Permission Opener
Another option for the Opener, is the "Tailored Permission Opener." This approach owns
the cold call and allows honesty to break the barrier.
Still lead with Context
• Hi Ben, I just read the press release about your IND for MS..
• Hi Sue, I saw your new office is opening in Boston..
Then, be honest.
• I'm going to be honest this is a cold call, but a well-researched one.
• I know I'm interrupting your day
Ask for permission to continue:
• Can I get 30 seconds to tell you why the press release prompted me to call you
specifically, then you can tell me it makes sense for us to continue talking or not?
◦^Give the out.
TIP
Don't sound overly chipper or intense, or their guard will stay up because you will sound like a
telemarketer who will pressure sell them no matter how they respond.
If you sound too scared or anxious, you'll seem too young/low-status for them to hear out,
especially if this is upper management.

DON'T PANIC.

Problem Propositions
You made it through the intro, now what?
Don't lead with value prop or buzzwords - find the problem!
People act on problems more than benefits.
Value has no context without a problem.
When I say don't lead with value or buzzwords - don't just dive into our Sanguine has a
direct-to-donor model where we can access 70,000 patients. Yes, that adds value, but why
is that relevant? This is where your research comes in.
Current Clients:
• Use what you know. What are some challenges those clients have faced? Check with
your Account Executive to help you build these pitches.
New Clients:
• Use what makes sense based on the company
You can break it down by Persona, Annoyances, or Emotion. Be specific.
Let's walk through a few scenarios:
1. Company focused in rare disease: Typically when I speak with researchers focused
on rare disease like XYZ, they have trouble getting diseased samples to work with and
they're wasting so much time. Is that something you run into?

TIP
Be careful using the same problems for different personas. Directors and Managers are not
going to be in the weeds like scientists and lower level researchers who are using the
samples.
Use Gong to listen to more customer problems on discovery calls.
Finish with a ONE SENTENCE SOLUTION. This has to be something that differentiates us
to solve the problem you shared.
For example: Most scientists working in translational research think it's frustrating to find
verified patient samples because they can't access donor clinical data from their current
vendors. --> Sanguine's direct-to-donor model allows us to obtain fully redacted medical
records and additional PROs with the samples to provide a complete donor profile.
Then an interest-based Call-to-Action:
• "Would you be open to learning more when I'm not completely calling you out of the
blue?"
• "My guess is you might be all set with samples for the rest of the year, but would you
be opposed to learning more as you work on your budgeting for next year?"
If they say yes, try and provide some time ranges, confirm their email live. Send a calendar
invite regardless.

Objection Handling
It seems counter intuitive, but if they say "We're all set" rather than diving into your pitch...
1. Agree with the objection
a. Not interested: Understood - you probably would have reached out if you really
needed samples!
b. No budget: I hear you, with all the cuts now adays its hard to add anything new
into the budget.
c. Competitor: I totally get that, a lot of people use them, and I guess if it's not broke
don't fix it.
d. Timing: Understood, I did call in the middle of your day!
2. Incentivize conversation
a. "Not interested": Just so no one calls you again, is it because you don't use
patient samples or you just hate cold calls?
b. No budget: Just so no one calls you again, is it because there is no budget for this
quarter or this year, or just because it's hard to add anything new to the budget?
c. Competitor: Just so no one calls you again, is it because you're happy with your
current provider or because it's a hassle to add new vendors?
d. Timing: Is something happening in the next 6 months or are you just swamped
right now?
3. Sell the test drive
a. Since you don't have a need right now, but if anything should ever change/budget
frees up, would you be open to taking a look at our offerings so you know what we

Voicemail
Should you leave a voicemail?
The goal of a voicemail isn't to get a callback. It's to get a reply over EMAIL.
1. If you do, lead with context like you would in the Opener section.
2. Direct back to the email
3. Incentivize Reply
4. Introduce yourself
Here's an example:
"Chelsea, we work with several of Abbvie's research teams at the Boston location. No need
to call me back I'm about to hit send on an email. Just so we don't play phone tag, would
you mind replying and letting me know if it is even moderately interesting? It'll come from
Jack at Sanguine. Cheers!"
TIP
1. Keep them REALLY SHORT
2. Only leave 2 VM's max per contact
3. In your follow up email, mirror your pitch you would have given!
4. Mention the voicemail in a follow up email ("just left you a voicemail, any thoughts?"

Conclusion
How to ace the Cold Call:
• Confidence in your voice - don't sound unsure or overly stiff.
• Imagine you're talking to a referral.
• Watch the upward inflection (making a statement sound like a ?)
• Don't read off a script
• Focus less on the words and more on the delivery
• The best time to call in the morning, late afternoon
• Accounts with Gatekeepers and Directories (that don't connect you!) might not be the
best time spent for your calls - try it once and then move on
Practice the objection and the talk track
1. Put together a Problem Proposition
2. Practice handling the most common objections
3. Prepare a couple examples WITHIN YOUR TERRITORY to practice on
