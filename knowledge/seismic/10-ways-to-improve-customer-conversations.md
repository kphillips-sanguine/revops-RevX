Introduction
Welcome!
In this lesson, we will review ten strategies you can use to improve customer
conversations. Sharing information and insights is vital in everyday life, but it is often
amplified in the business world. Even though we learn to converse at an early age, there is
always room for us to improve our communication skills.
Topics covered:
• Paying attention and effective listening
• Being open-minded and avoiding assumptions
• Asking the right questions

Tip 1: Don't Multitask
If you want to get out of a conversation, then get out. Don't be half in and half out
of a conversation.
– Celeste Headlee
Avoid multitasking and be present.
Multitasking indicates you aren't fully present in the moment. When speaking with a
customer, you are multitasking if you are doing something else at the same time such as

---

<!-- Page 6 -->
looking at your cell phone, sending messages to a coworker, or trying to complete another
work task.
You are also multitasking if you're thinking about something else, like an earlier argument
with your boss, deciding where you want to go to dinner, or reminiscing about some
summer you spent on a beach.
Multitasking interferes with conversations.
Customers want to feel heard. It’s difficult for even the most experienced agents to sound
engaged and helpful when trying to do anything but listen to the customer. This is true for
any form of customer interaction, such as phone and chat.
Refraining from multitasking can be difficult for customer support agents who have the
double duty of listening to a customer while also trying to solve their problem as quickly as
possible.
Listening should always be the priority because it will help you identify and
resolve customer needs effectively and efficiently, while also building strong
relationships with customers.

Tip 2: Be Open-Minded
You need to enter every conversation assuming that you have something to
learn…sometimes that means setting aside your personal opinion.
– Celeste Headlee
Keep an open mind.
As a customer support representative, you should never enter a conversation with
assumptions. Each customer is different, and their individual needs should be heard in

---

<!-- Page 8 -->
order to provide the best service possible.
Listen to what the customer is telling you before trying to share your own point of
view.
Ensure you understand the customer by checking in with them. Summarize what
you heard them say and ask if you got it right.
Look for what you can learn from customers. Each customer has a unique
perspective and if you listen carefully, you can learn from each person and each
interaction. You can gain insights about their needs, the business, your work, and
even yourself.
Avoid speaking or expressing your opinions in a pompous way because that would
be off-putting to your customers. Try your best to be humble and open-minded
when you communicate!
Everyone you will ever meet knows something you don't.
– Bill Nye, The Science Guy

Tip 3: Use Open-Ended Questions
Take a cue from journalists. Start your questions with who, what, when, where, why
or how. ... Let them describe it. They’re the ones that know.
– Celeste Headlee
What are open-ended questions?
The type of question you ask will determine the type of answer you receive. Open-ended
questions typically cannot be answered with a simple "yes" or "no."

---

<!-- Page 10 -->
Journalists have been using this method for ages to get interviewees to give more
descriptive answers. Support agents should be versed in asking open-ended questions that
invite customers to expound on their issues, providing useful information that will help you
resolve the situation.
Here is an example:
Flip the card to see an open-ended alternative.
Yes or no question: “Are you having an issue installing our application onto your iOS
device?”
Open-ended question: “What can I help you with today?”

Tip 4: Go With the Flow
Thoughts will come into your mind, and you need to let them go. I've heard
interviews in which a guest is talking for several minutes and then the host asks a
question which seems like it comes out of nowhere. This means the host probably
stopped listening two minutes ago.
– Celeste Headlee

---

<!-- Page 12 -->
Stay engaged and efficient.
While there are some talking points that must be covered in a customer support interaction,
going with the flow of the conversation can be a good way to dive deeper into the issue.
In addition to making it seem like the support agent isn’t paying attention to the
conversation, stopping to constantly refer back to stock questions can unnecessarily
lengthen the amount of time spent in the customer interaction.

---

Tip 5: If You Don't Know, Say You Don't Know
People on the radio are much more aware that they’re going on the record, and so
they’re more careful about what they claim to be an expert in and what they claim
to know for sure. Do that. Err on the side of caution. Talk should not be cheap.
– Celeste Headlee
Take time to find the right answer.
It is okay for support representatives to take a moment and make sure they find the proper

---

<!-- Page 14 -->
solution. Don't claim to be an expert in something you don't know. The time it takes to
pause and look for information or transfer the customer to another agent who can help
them better will almost always be shorter than giving the customer the wrong answer and
sending them on their way.

Tip 6: Don't Equate Experiences
If they’re talking about the trouble they’re having at work, don’t tell them about how
much you hate your job. It’s not the same. It is never the same. All experiences are
individual. And, more importantly, it is not about you. Conversations are not a
promotional opportunity.
– Celeste Headlee

---

<!-- Page 18 -->
Remember that all experiences are individual.
People don't have the same experience. Even if you're trying to connect by sharing a
similar experience, your focus should be on the customer's experience.
But don't similar experiences help us connect?
It can be tempting to try to demonstrate that you understand a customer by sharing a similar
experience you have had. Comments like these don’t generally sound helpful or comforting to
the person in need of assistance because you will never have the exact same experience as
them or know exactly how they feel. Plus, it's not about you! Shifting the conversation to your
experience can feel invalidating and alienate the customer.

Tip 7: Try Not to Repeat Yourself
Why is repeating yourself bad?
Repeating yourself can be condescending and boring, yet we tend to do it a lot. When
someone has a point to make, they tend to rephrase that point over and over. Avoid doing
this because the repetition can actually undermine your point and cause the listener to
disengage.
Unless it is for something that needs absolute clarity, repeating phrases too many

---

<!-- Page 20 -->
times sounds like inattentiveness to the customer.

Tip 8: Stay Out of the Weeds
Stay on target.
Focus on solving the problem. The details aren't as important as the solution. Customers
don't want to be tied up in interactions with support agents. They simply want the
information needed to solve their problem.
How do you avoid the weeds?
• Understand the problem and relay the information needed to solve it.

---

<!-- Page 22 -->
• Spend enough time with the customer to fulfill their needs.

Tip 9: Listen
I cannot tell you how many really important people have said that listening is
perhaps the most, the number one most important skill that you could develop.
– Celeste Headlee
Active listening is essential.
Listening is a key component to having a better conversation. It is the most important skill
that any support team could develop. Listen with intent and demonstrate attentiveness.

---

<!-- Page 24 -->
Show you are a good listener by:
Understanding: When listening to customers, your purpose should be to
understand. Understand what they are saying, their needs, and the issue they are
describing. Don't assume that you understand everything customers share with you.
They are the experts, so restate what you heard and ask them to confirm if you've
understood correctly.
Empathizing: Humans want to feel cared for. By putting yourself in the customer's
place and sharing their feelings, you will validate their experience and open the door
to an effective conversation.
Being aware of your tone: Try to convey warmth and friendliness. Be mindful of
your speaking speed, pitch, and volume to create the impression you are trying to
create during a conversation.
Showing interest: Be curious and seek to learn from each interaction. Use follow-
up questions to get the information you need.
Being present: You can't listen effectively if you are not paying attention. Try your
best to avoid distractions and if your mind drifts, ask for clarification.

<!-- Page 25 -->
Tip 10: Be Brief
All of this boils down to the same basic concept, and it is this one: Be interested in
other people...
– Celeste Headlee
Speak just enough.
A conversation with a customer is not an opportunity for you to express yourself. The focus
should be on the customer and their satisfaction, so you should only say what is necessary

---

<!-- Page 26 -->
to resolve their issue and give them a positive interaction. This doesn't mean you can't
sound natural and human, but try to ask yourself:
Is what I am saying necessary, helpful, and kind?
