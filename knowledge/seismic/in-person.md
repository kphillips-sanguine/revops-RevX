Introduction
In-person meetings are crucial for building strong client relationships, which can lead to
larger deals, higher win rates, and faster sales cycles. However, planning and executing
these meetings can be challenging due to geographical and scheduling limitations.
Leveraging local resources, strategically planning travel, and using cold calling techniques
can help overcome these challenges and maximize the effectiveness of in-person meetings.
This lesson will provide practical tips and strategies for successfully planning and
conducting in-person meetings to strengthen client rapport and drive business growth.

Geographical limitations
Geographical Limitations: How to plan around these
Those who don't live locally need to plan their trips strategically:
• Land one big meeting and schedule other meetings around that one
• Geo target for those meetings
• Use the Salesforce Mapping tool to see what locations are close by to your big
meeting
• Send out cadences to those clients saying you will be in the area and see if they're
available to meet
• Plan this several weeks in advance if possible so you have time to schedule other
meetings and make your trip worth it
• Plan a larger strategic event planned around a topic (something that will bring interest
to the client - for example: How Patient Data impacts Research - connect with
marketing for other ideas)
◦Customer Dinner
◦Happy Hour Event
*Note: You aren't going to want to offer to bring lunch or something to every single client or
you won't be able to go to multiple clients each day; If you have a lot of big clients, see if
they would be open to an event as mentioned above, or just try and do lunch or coffee with
your closest contact and ask to stop by for others.

Local Travel
Schedule multiple meetings per day if possible to maximize your time in the area
Choose a location (coffee shop) and invite clients to meet you there
In cadences and conversations, start asking for in-person meetings
• I'm local to the area and would love to discuss your specimen needs over coffee. Do
you have a few minutes on Tuesday or Thursday morning?
• I'll be in the area on Wednesday:
• Would it make sense to review the proposal together in person?
• Would it make sense for us to meet for coffee and review the I/E together?
• Do you have a few minutes to meet briefly and discuss this in person? Let me know if
morning or afternoon works better for you.
For certain accounts where you land one meeting:
Message other people at the account who have responded to you in the past, I'm meeting
with a colleague at X account name on Thursday. Do you have a few minutes to meet while
I'm at your office? I'd love to put a face to the name.

Tips
Tips:
• Block off your calendar for travel to ensure your SDR doesn't book meetings when you
are traveling
• Plan for in person meetings to run over the scheduled time (as they typically do)
• Keep an eye on the time if you have multiple meetings and need to leave by a certain
time (let them know!)
• Plan to arrive a few minutes early for security checkpoints
• Look up parking tips in advance if you are driving!
• If you have a MAC, make sure you bring your HDMI adapter to connect to their
projectors!!!

Cold Calling
Cold Calling In Person
If you get into the building for one meeting and there are other biotech in the same building,
look up a contact or two at the account that you have reached out to previously
Send them a quick message: I'm going to be in the area and wanted to drop off XYZ (ex
brochure that highlights our new capabilities). Do you have a few minutes around X time?
Ask the secretary if that person is available to meet - or drop off a flier.
If you drop off a flier, send a follow up email to say something along the lines of: I was in
the area today, and must have caught you at a bad time, but I dropped off a flier with XYZ
value. Please let me know if you have a few minutes (next week or whenever you plan to go
back to the area, or try to schedule another meeting).
This at least shows effort and may go a long way
I wouldn't do this for large pharma, unless you already had a meeting and were just leaving
something at the front desk for another person at the account
Their security is too high and might not go over well.
What should you do if there are other biotech companies in the same building as your
meeting?
Look up a contact at the account you have reached out to previously and send them a quick
message about dropping off XYZ.

Dress Code & Temperment
Dress code should be business casual or professional
• Do not wear jeans, t-shirts, sweatshirts
• Do wear: Dress pants, blouse, button down, polo, dress shoes
You should look polished and professional for all client facing interactions.
Be aware of your Body Language
• Silence your phones
• Focus on your client - don't be distracted
• Check the volume and pace of your voice
◦Some people get too quiet in person, Others get too loud
◦Some people go to slow, others go too fast
◦Read the room and match the energy
Be aware of the CLIENT's Body Language
• See if they are engaged
• If they seem shy, make sure you help run the conversation and don't leave gaps

Small Talk
As you begin in person meetings, they can be a little awkward at first. You're in a new
space. Clients aren't often used to hosting vendors.
Small talk is very important in these situations - especially as you're walking through
security, touring their facility, going in the elevator. You must be able to make conversation.
For example:
• How long have they been in this building?
• Are they coming in every day? Or just a few days a week?
• If you're also local - seeing where they live ("Do you live close by or do you have a long
commute?")
• If close to the weekend - weekend plans?
• Bring up something you know about them - kids, sports etc.
• Weather
• Random story about their visit
Be appreciative of them hosting you - thank them for having you.
Reminder, some things that are typically not appropriate to discuss with clients, as
they can be controversial subjects:
1. Politics
2. Religion
3. Finances

Taking Control of the Conversation
When you're in person, you need to dial up your active listening, but you also need to
maintain control of the conversation and guide it accordingly.
Active Listening
• Ensure you're listening to the client
• Nodding along
• Making eye contact
• Jotting down notes when applicable
• Ask clarifying questions
• Ask more questions about a topic and relate it back to Sanguine's services as
applicable
People will open up more in person. They will share things you wouldn't typically hear in a
Zoom call. For example:
"You can finish up here, I have a 2pm with BioIVT I don't want to keep them waiting." Well,
now we know who our competitor is.
"Sanguine is actually not on the top vendor list after our last project." Ask more clarifying
questions and figure out how to rectify.
"Bill will be joining" Managers sometimes hop in last minute!
You have to be ready for anything they throw at you, while maintaining calm and

Preparing
As you typically prepare for a meeting, you want to come extra prepared for an in-person
meeting. New people will show up. Different contacts will come in. Old studies can be
referenced.
• Refresh on all studies
• Know key contacts names
• Anything current in the pipeline
• Ordering process - MSA, third party platform...
• CDA in place?
Presentation Preparation
• Download everything in advance, especially if you can't connect to WiFi
• There is a chance you won't be able to connect to the monitor - you should be able to
talk about all of Sanguine's services/product updates without your material

Post Meeting
You did it. You got the meeting. Learned a lot from the conversation. Now what?
• Send follow up materials within 24 hours of visit (Digital Sales Room in Seismic is great
for sharing!)
• Ensure you say THANK YOU for hosting you
• Include all members you met with on the follow up, or ask your host for their info or to
share the material, depending on the information
• Add to Nurture Cadence - if no immediate opportunity
• Be sure to connect via LinkedIn (if haven't already)
• Update Account Plan with note

Conclusion
In-person meetings hold significant value in building strong client relationships and driving
business growth, especially in the biotech industry. Although challenges like geographical
