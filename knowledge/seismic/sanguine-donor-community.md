Autoimmune
Autoimmune
Autoimmune conditions make up the majority of our database. The majority of these
conditions are chronic, so our donors have had this diagnosis for a long time and will have
it forever, although severity may change.
Treatment may include medications such as corticosteroids, immunosuppressants,
biologics, and disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs.
• If they do want to exclude corticosteroids, it's important to ask in what timeframe? The
past 30 days? 60 days?
Diagnosis: Diagnosing autoimmune diseases can be challenging because symptoms often
overlap with other conditions ("co-morbidities"). Healthcare professionals typically use a
combination of medical history, physical examination, blood tests to detect autoantibodies,
imaging tests, and biopsies to diagnose autoimmune diseases.
• It's common for donors with one autoimmune disease to have multiple co-morbidities,
meaning they have more than one disease.
◦Excluding other autoimmune conditions is likely not feasible
• It's common for certain conditions to have anemia, which may result in lower blood
volumes. Check the Conditions Summary Sheet for relevant anemia information and
blood volumes
• Steroids - exclude, for what time frame?

Rare Disease
Rare Disease
Rare diseases are medical conditions that affect a small number of people compared to the
general population. Since we only collect within the Continental US, the ability to find these
patients can be challenging.
We utilize Patient Advocacy Organizations as the main source of recruitment for these
patients. They can help recruit by sharing email blasts about the study, posting on their
social media channels, in their newsletters etc. This is typically an effective approach but
one of the challenges are:
• we can't guarantee the advocacy group will want to work with us
• we can't guarantee how many patients will sign up so it's recommended to start with a
smaller number of patients until we can prove success
• they typically need the client to disclose their name and research purpose
◦the client will likely need to get legal approval to share this information
◦if the client hasn't publicly disclosed they are working on this indication, they will
likely not be able to share
• adding i/e to rare disease studies can potentially make them unfeasible

Neurological Disorders
Neurological diseases
Neurological diseases, also known as neurological disorders or nervous system disorders,
encompass a wide range of conditions that affect the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral
nerves. These diseases can vary widely in terms of their causes, symptoms, severity, and
treatment options.
Types: Neurological diseases can be broadly categorized into various subtypes, including:
• Neurodegenerative diseases: Examples include Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's
disease, Huntington's disease, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS).
◦These are challenging because the donors can progress quickly, they are forgetful
and may miss appointments or forget to sign consent forms, or may need a
caregiver
◦Certain stages make them unfeasible - always check the Conditions for Feasibility
Weekly
• Movement disorders: Such as essential tremor, dystonia, and restless legs syndrome.
• Stroke and cerebrovascular diseases: Including ischemic stroke, hemorrhagic stroke,
and transient ischemic attack (TIA).
• Epilepsy and seizure disorders: Characterized by recurrent seizures.
• Headaches and migraines: Chronic conditions that cause severe headaches.
• Neurodevelopmental disorders: Such as autism spectrum disorder, attention-deficit/
hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), and intellectual disabilities.
• Multiple sclerosis (MS) and other demyelinating diseases: Involving damage to the

---

<!-- Page 11 -->
protective myelin sheath around nerve fibers.
• Neuromuscular diseases: Such as muscular dystrophy, myasthenia gravis, and
peripheral neuropathy.
• Neuroinflammatory diseases: Including meningitis, encephalitis, and multiple sclerosis.
• Traumatic brain injury (TBI) and spinal cord injury (SCI): Resulting from physical trauma
to the brain or spinal cord.
Diagnosis:
Diagnosing neurological diseases often involves a combination of medical history, physical
examination, neurological tests, imaging studies (such as MRI or CT scans), and laboratory
tests (such as blood tests or cerebrospinal fluid analysis).
Challenges
• Many clients may as for MRI or CT scan results, which can sometimes be hard to
obtain based on our medical record retrieval (may change with FIHR) and our MR

Oncology
Oncology
Oncology or cancer is one of the challenging communities for our standard recruitment
methods.
Types of Cancer: There are more than 100 different types of cancer, which can affect
virtually any part of the body. For research, we typically get requests for:
• Solid Tumor Cancers (Breast cancer, lung cancer)
• Liquid Tumor Cancers (AML, Leukemia) that attack the blood
Diagnosis: Cancer diagnosis typically involves a combination of medical history, physical
examination, imaging tests (such as X-rays, CT scans, MRI scans, and PET scans),
laboratory tests (such as blood tests and tumor markers), and tissue biopsies (to examine
cells under a microscope).
Treatment: Treatment options for cancer depend on various factors, including the type and
stage of cancer, the donor's overall health and preferences, and the goals of treatment.
Common treatment modalities include surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted
therapy, immunotherapy, hormone therapy, stem cell transplantation, and palliative care to
manage symptoms and improve quality of life.
Active vs Remission
Active oncology means the patient current is in one of the active stages of their cancer.
They are likely undergoing treatment. Trying to find active oncology donors has proven to
be very difficult for us to recruit because most donors during this time are not actively
looking for research, they are looking for a cure.

---

<!-- Page 13 -->
Our competitors who use clinicians are able to identify these donors faster pre-treatment
and post-treatment, as the patients are consistently going in for doctor appointments and
treatments. It is a very small window to find donors, and unfortunately getting pre-treated
samples not feasible for us at this time.
Another element about active oncology is we constantly have to be recruiting NEW donors
for every study. We can't rely on our database because the donors' condition changes so
frequently.
Remission
Remission oncology donors are much more engaged through our typical recruitment
methods which means we have a higher likelihood of finding remission donors. However,
Oncology still does remain more challenging for us and may be more expensive than our
competitors. We do have some value adds with our medical records and ability to survey
these donors (PROs) for additional information and do longitudinal collections.
Challenges
• Specific treatment plans - especially newer treatment (Immunotherapy, Car-T)
• Active oncology

Genetic
Genetic Diseases
Genetic diseases, also known as inherited diseases or genetic disorders, are conditions
caused by abnormalities in an individual's genetic material. These abnormalities can occur
in the DNA sequence of genes, affecting their structure or function. Genetic diseases can
be inherited from one or both parents, or they can arise spontaneously due to new
mutations. Here's an overview of genetic diseases:
1. Types of Genetic Diseases:
• Single-Gene Disorders: These disorders are caused by mutations in a single gene
and typically follow a predictable inheritance pattern, such as autosomal dominant,
autosomal recessive, or X-linked.
• Chromosomal Disorders: These disorders result from abnormalities in the
structure or number of chromosomes. Examples include Down syndrome (trisomy
21) and Turner syndrome (monosomy X).
• Multifactorial Disorders: These disorders are influenced by both genetic and
environmental factors. Examples include heart disease, diabetes, and certain types
of cancer.
• Mitochondrial Disorders: These disorders are caused by mutations in
mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), which is inherited exclusively from the mother.
Mitochondrial disorders can affect various organs and systems in the body.
2. Common Genetic Diseases we work with:
• Cystic Fibrosis: Affecting the respiratory and digestive systems, cystic fibrosis is
caused by mutations in the CFTR gene.

---

<!-- Page 15 -->
◦Common for <18
• Sickle Cell Disease: This inherited blood disorder is caused by mutations in the
HBB gene, resulting in abnormal hemoglobin and red blood cells.
◦Knowing the genetic element for SCD is more common than most diseases,
specifically HbSS
• Huntington's Disease: A neurodegenerative disorder caused by a mutation in the
HTT gene, leading to progressive deterioration of cognitive and motor functions.
• Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy: This progressive muscle-wasting disorder is
caused by mutations in the DMD gene, resulting in the absence of the protein
dystrophin.
◦Almost all study requests are for <18 years
3. Symptoms and Manifestations:
• Symptoms of genetic diseases vary widely depending on the specific disorder but
may include developmental delays, intellectual disability, physical abnormalities,
organ dysfunction, and susceptibility to certain medical conditions.
• Symptoms can range from mild to severe and may present at birth or later in life,
depending on factors such as the type of genetic mutation and its impact on gene
function.
4. Diagnosis and Management:
• Diagnosis of genetic diseases often involves a combination of family history
assessment, clinical evaluation, genetic testing, and molecular analysis.
• Management of genetic diseases may include medical interventions to alleviate
symptoms, supportive care, lifestyle modifications, and in some cases, gene
therapy or other emerging treatments aimed at addressing the underlying genetic

---

<!-- Page 16 -->
cause.
• Genetic counseling plays a crucial role in providing individuals and families with
information about the inheritance pattern, recurrence risks, and available options
for family planning and genetic testing.
5. Research and Treatment Advances:
• Ongoing research in genetics, genomics, and molecular biology continues to
deepen our understanding of genetic diseases and pave the way for the
development of targeted therapies and precision medicine approaches.
• Advances in technologies such as next-generation sequencing (NGS), CRISPR
gene editing, and gene therapy hold promise for the future treatment and
prevention of genetic diseases.
Challenges:

Infectious Disease & Exposure
Infectious Disease
Infectious diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses,
fungi, or parasites. These diseases can be transmitted directly or indirectly from person to
person, through contact with contaminated surfaces, food, water, or vectors like
mosquitoes or ticks. Here's an overview of infectious diseases:
1. Types of Infectious Agents:
• Bacteria: Bacterial infections can cause a wide range of illnesses, including strep
throat, tuberculosis, urinary tract infections, and bacterial pneumonia.
◦Not feasible
• Viruses: Viral infections are responsible for diseases such as the common cold,
influenza (flu), HIV/AIDS, hepatitis, measles, and COVID-19.
◦Not feasible for current viral infection (cold, flu, covid)
◦Recommended longitudinal for vaccine studies
• Fungi: Fungal infections can affect the skin, nails, respiratory tract, and internal
organs, leading to conditions such as athlete's foot, ringworm, candidiasis, and
invasive fungal infections.
◦Most are not feasible
• Parasites: Parasitic infections, transmitted through contaminated food or water or
by insect vectors, include malaria, toxoplasmosis, giardiasis, and intestinal worms.
◦Not feasible

---

<!-- Page 18 -->
2. Transmission Modes:
• Direct Contact: Transmission occurs through physical contact with an infected
person, such as through touching, kissing, or sexual contact.
• Indirect Contact: Transmission occurs via contact with contaminated surfaces,
objects, or fluids, such as respiratory droplets, blood, or feces.
• Vector-borne Transmission: Certain diseases are transmitted by arthropod
vectors such as mosquitoes (e.g., malaria, dengue fever), ticks (e.g., Lyme
disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever), or fleas (e.g., bubonic plague).
• Foodborne and Waterborne Transmission: Ingestion of contaminated food or
water can lead to gastrointestinal infections caused by bacteria, viruses, or
parasites.
3. Clinical Manifestations:
• Symptoms of infectious diseases vary depending on the specific pathogen and
affected organ systems but may include fever, fatigue, cough, diarrhea, vomiting,
rash, joint pain, and respiratory symptoms.
• Some infectious diseases may have mild, self-limiting symptoms, while others can
cause severe illness, organ damage, or death.
4. Diagnosis and Treatment:
• Diagnosis of infectious diseases involves clinical evaluation, laboratory tests (e.g.,
blood tests, cultures, molecular tests), imaging studies, and sometimes serological
testing to detect antibodies.
• Treatment of infectious diseases depends on the causative agent and may include
antimicrobial medications (e.g., antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals), supportive care,
and symptom management.
• Antimicrobial resistance is a growing concern, necessitating judicious use of

---

<!-- Page 19 -->
antibiotics and the development of alternative treatment strategies.
For Infectious diseases such as HIV, HBV, HCV:
• HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) itself is not considered an acute infectious
disease. Instead, it causes a chronic infection that progressively weakens the immune
system over time, leading to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) if left
untreated. However, HIV infection can present with an acute phase known as acute
HIV infection or acute retroviral syndrome (ARS).
• Hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection can manifest as either acute or chronic disease,
depending on the duration of the infection and the body's immune response.
◦Acute: Short-term (few weeks to several months) - not as feasible as chronic
◦Chronic: Virus persists in the body for more than six months.
• Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is a blood-borne virus that primarily affects the liver, leading to
inflammation (hepatitis); Can be acute or chronic, like HBV.
Challenges:
• Many infectious diseases have co-infections
• Must ask if they need active, acute or chronic

Cardiovasucular
1. Types of Cardiovascular Diseases:
• Coronary Artery Disease (CAD): CAD occurs when the blood vessels that supply
oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle become narrowed or blocked due to the
buildup of plaque (atherosclerosis). This can lead to chest pain (angina), heart
attacks (myocardial infarction), and other complications.
◦Put in ICs for these. Some are feasible, others are not.
• Stroke: A stroke occurs when the blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted
or reduced, leading to brain damage. Ischemic strokes, caused by blood clots
blocking arteries in the brain, are the most common type.
◦When did the stroke occur? We would have to get a range?
• Heart Failure: Heart failure occurs when the heart is unable to pump enough
blood to meet the body's needs. It can result from conditions such as CAD, high
blood pressure, or damage to the heart muscle (e.g., from a heart attack).
• Arrhythmias: Arrhythmias are abnormal heart rhythms that can cause the heart to
beat too fast, too slow, or irregularly. They can increase the risk of complications
such as stroke or sudden cardiac arrest.
• Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD): PAD occurs when plaque buildup narrows the
arteries in the limbs, usually the legs. It can cause leg pain, numbness, and
difficulty walking, and it increases the risk of complications such as infections or
limb amputation.
2. Diagnosis:
• Diagnosis of CVD may involve a combination of medical history, physical
examination, laboratory tests (such as blood tests to measure cholesterol levels),

---

<!-- Page 22 -->
imaging tests (such as echocardiography or cardiac MRI), and specialized tests
(such as stress tests or coronary angiography) to assess heart function and blood
flow.
3. Treatment:
• Treatment of CVD aims to manage symptoms, reduce the risk of complications,
and improve overall heart health. Treatment may include lifestyle modifications
(such as diet and exercise), medications (such as statins, blood pressure
medications, or anticoagulants), medical procedures (such as angioplasty or
coronary artery bypass surgery), and cardiac rehabilitation programs.

Metabolic
1. Metabolic Disorders:
• Metabolic disorders are conditions characterized by abnormalities in metabolism,
resulting from genetic mutations, enzyme deficiencies, or other factors.
• Examples of metabolic disorders include:
◦Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes is a group of metabolic disorders characterized
by high blood sugar levels (hyperglycemia) due to defects in insulin secretion,
insulin action, or both. Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition resulting
in the destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, while type 2
diabetes involves insulin resistance and impaired insulin secretion.
◦Obesity: Obesity is a metabolic disorder characterized by excessive
accumulation of body fat, often resulting from a combination of genetic,
environmental, and lifestyle factors. Obesity is associated with an increased
risk of various health complications, including type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular
disease, and certain cancers.
◦Hyperlipidemia: Hyperlipidemia refers to elevated levels of lipids (fats) in the
blood, including cholesterol and triglycerides. It is a risk factor for
cardiovascular disease, as high levels of cholesterol can lead to the buildup of
plaque in the arteries (atherosclerosis).
◦Inherited Metabolic Disorders: These are genetic disorders caused by
mutations in genes encoding enzymes or other proteins involved in metabolic
pathways. Examples include phenylketonuria (PKU), galactosemia, and
lysosomal storage disorders such as Gaucher disease and Pompe disease.
▪These are also rare diseases; Put in ICs for feasibility assessment

2. Diagnosis and Management:
• Diagnosis of metabolic disorders typically involves a combination of medical
history, physical examination, laboratory tests (such as blood tests, genetic
testing, and imaging studies), and specialized metabolic tests to assess enzyme
activity or metabolite levels.
• Management of metabolic disorders may include dietary modifications, lifestyle
changes, medications (such as insulin or lipid-lowering drugs), enzyme
replacement therapy, and gene therapy for certain inherited metabolic disorders.
For some metabolic diseases that affect kidneys, it will impact the amount of blood we can
