Lab Processing Overview
Long-term storage of whole blood is impractical due to quick degradation (nucleic acid,
protein and cell) in the presence of cell- lytic enzymes, even at low temperature. Because
of this, researchers may need to either use whole blood and leukopak fresh, or have a fresh
collection processed as quickly as possible into a form suitable for long-term storage in
liquid nitrogen (LN or LN2).
The types of processing required by our customer primarily include blood component
isolation (i.e. serum, plasma, PBMC and subpopulations of PBMC), sample aliquoting and
cryopreservation. Currently, we have the capability to process:
• Whole blood - plasma, serum, PBMCs, cell isolations
• Leukopaks
• Urine
• Stool (cryopreservation only)
• Skin tape (cryopreservation only)
For information on additional samples types or complicated requirements, contact the
feasibility team for assessment.

Timing of Processing
Next Day
Lab processing is an important pillar of our business. Since prospective collection involves
in-home visits and overnight shipping, we offer next day processing as our standard

Cryopreservation & Quality Equipment
• Cryopreservation
◦Freezers - The service lab is equipped with freezers with various temperature
ratings (-20°C and -80°C).
▪Generally, -20°C freezer is used to store swabs, skin tapes, nail clips and
blood cards.
▪Large volume biospecimens (i.e. serum, plasma, urine, stool, etc.) requiring
mid- to long-term storage are stored in the -80°C freezer. -80°C is comparable

Serum / Plasma Isolation
• Serum Processing
Serum is the liquid component of blood that remains after blood has clotted. It consists of
water, electrolytes, antibodies, antigens, hormones, and any exogenous substances (e.g.,
drugs) present in the blood. Serum is often used in medical diagnostics and research to
analyze various components of blood.
Serum isolation can be done in-home, as well as processed by our lab team. Processing
whole blood for serum is relatively straightforward. After clotting (~30 min), which occurs
spontaneously inside of the blood tube, the tubes are centrifuged once. Two layers should
form inside the blood tubes. The upper, light yellow layer is serum.
Many factors may have an impact on the ratio of serum in blood. To our donors, a few
notable factors include the medical condition, age, and hydration status. Generally, we use
1:3 serum to blood ratio to calculate required blood volume, which means for 10mL serum,
30mL whole blood should be collected.
For serum isolation, whole blood must be collected in Serum Separation Tubes (SST) only,
which contains no anti-coagulant. Aliquoted serum is shipped on dry ice overnight by
default, or stored at -80°C in a freezer.
• Plasma Processing
In comparison to serum, plasma is isolated from un-clotted whole blood. During the
processing, only the cellular component from blood is removed, Everything seen in serum,
as well as the components lost due to coagulation, are retained in plasma.
While serum is suitable for a broader range of applications in research, including

---

<!-- Page 14 -->
immunoassays, enzyme activity assays, and biomarker studies. Plasma is preferred for
certain types of analyses, such as those requiring measurement of coagulation
factors or drug levels.
Plasma isolation is also a feasible in-home procedure. Unlike serum isolation, blood tubes
can be spun down immediately after collection, because of the presence of anti-coagulant,
factors (proteins, platelets, etc.) that are involved in clotting remain soluble in the plasma.
After centrifugation, like serum isolation, plasma is harvested from the upper layer as well.
The ratio of plasma in blood also varies for a number of reasons (same as serum). Since
more blood components are retained in plasma compared to serum, a 1:2 plasma to blood
ratio is used to calculate the required blood volume. So, 10mL plasma can be expected
from 20mL whole blood.
Common anti-coagulants seen in blood tubes include EDTA, heparin and Acid Citrate
Dextrose (ACD). The choice of anti-coagulant depends on the downstream applications
because these chemicals may affect certain assays but show tolerance to others. Aliquoted
plasma is shipped on dry ice overnight by default, or stored at -80°C in a freezer.
• Double-Spun Plasma
The double-spun procedure is applied when Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) or platelet-poor
plasma (PPP) is ordered. Unlike serum and plasma isolation described above, PRP and
PPP isolation require two rounds of centrifugation. This is usually done by spinning the
tube with whole blood at a lower speed to separate RBCs and WBCs from the plasma first.
Due to the low density of platelets, most of the platelets would remain in the plasma
fraction. This is followed by a second round of spinning at high speed to condense the
platelets toward the bottom of the plasma fraction. When PPP is required, the top 2/3 of
the plasma (not the entire content of the tube) is harvested. In the case of PRP isolation, we
would retain the the bottom 1/3 of the plasma and discard the tops 2/3.

PBMC Isolation
PBMCs
Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) are a diverse population of immune cells
found in the blood, including lymphocytes (T cells, B cells, and Natural Killer cells) and
monocytes. PBMCs play a crucial role in the body's immune response, serving as key
mediators of adaptive and innate immunity. These cells are involved in recognizing and
eliminating pathogens, producing antibodies, and regulating immune responses. PBMCs
are widely used in biomedical research and clinical applications for studying immune
function, autoimmune diseases, infectious diseases, cancer immunotherapy, and vaccine
development.
Although PBMC can be subdivided into the aforementioned cell types (T, B, NK cells and
monocytes), it is a subpopulation of WBCs, which also include granulocytes that take up
~50% of all WBCs. Therefore, PBMC isolation is a process that removes all RBCs,
platelets, granulocytes, as well as plasma from the whole blood. In our lab, isolation of
PBMCs from blood samples is typically performed using density gradient centrifugation.
Common density gradient medium seen in the market include Ficoll-Paque™ (Cytiva) and
Lymphoprep™ (Stemcell), both offered at a density of 1.077 g/mL. We use Lymphoprep as
our default option. However, isolation with Ficoll is also available upon request. No
apparent performance difference is observed.
Generally, we use a ratio of 1e6 PBMC in every mL of whole blood to convert between
PBMC count and blood volume. However, we have noticed that the PBMC yield tend to be
lower in some disease state samples, especially the auto-immune diseases. It is
advised to consult with the feasibility or science team for better estimations in those cases.
Although all cell types have been found of therapeutic value, we are currently limited to

Buffy Coat
Buffy coat is essentially a white blood cell (WBC) and platelet mixture that is fractionated
after spinning down whole blood treated with anti-coagulant. It is a thin, whitish-gray layer
of cells that forms between the plasma and red blood cells in a blood sample after
centrifugation. Unlike the PBMC isolation by density gradient centrifugation. the buffy coat
is in direct contact with the RBC layer after centrifugation, instead of being segregated by
the density gradient medium. To harvest the buffy coat, the top plasma layer must be
removed first, with only 1-2mL of plasma left behind so the buffy coat layer remains intact.
This is followed by careful aspiration of the buffy coat layer with the remaining plasma,
while minimizing carry over of the bottom RBC layer.
With the technique described above, it is inevitable to see RBC contamination in a buffy
coat collection, so subsequent RBC lysis is necessary. Also, depending on the experience
level of the technician, the resulting volume could be different as it is directly related to
one's pipetting skill.
It is worth noting that some customers tend to ask how much buffy coat we can collect.
This is a tricky (and non-scientific) question due to the reasons above. A large volume of
buffy coat is not an indicator for higher number of WBCs, but rather a suggestion of the
personal pipetting preference of the technician as well as his/her skill level. Nonetheless, as
general guideline, we expect 1-2mL of buffy coat collected from every 100mL of whole
blood.
With PBMC gaining popularity, researchers have shifted their interest to PBMC that is
predominantly lymphocytes used in cell therapy. Our customers may still request buffy
coat, sometimes with a real need, other times just a habit of using an old vocabulary. We
should always bear in mind that buffy coat isolation is not a preferred service to quote on,
and proactively qualify the lead by asking the specific cell type of interest, then encourage

Cryopreservation
Cryopreservation is the last step of sample processing and it plays a vital role on cell
viability post-thaw. In the case of PBMC cryopreservation, PBMCs are gently washed
before diluting to desired concentration in a pre-chilled cryopreservation medium. Cells at
this stage have gone through long hours of shipping and handling in a non-physiological
environment, then stored in a buffer deprived of serum, so it is important to minimize the
waiting time while avoid introducing any sudden stimulation.
An important step in PBMC cryopreservation is controlled-rate freezing. PBMC aliquots
must be gradually frozen at a rate of -1°C/min before transferring to liquid nitrogen for long-
term storage. This is achieved by placing the PBMC vials in a controlled-rate freezing
device and leaving them undisturbed in a -80°C freezer for at least 4 hours or overnight.
Snap freezing will result in crystallization of water and protein molecules inside of cells,
which may rupture cells and cause loss of viability.
By default, for translation studies, we resuspend the final cell pellet in a cryopreservation
medium at 1e6 M/mL, and aliquot 1 mL of cell suspension in each 2 mL cryovial. Custom
cell number in each vial is possible, but must be accepted by Lab Ops team through
submitting a Lab Application. Our standard is 10M per vial.

Customization
The ability to accept custom processing requirements is a key differentiator of our product
& service. Understanding what can be customized and why customers ask for
customizations could add more weight to our offering. Here are the major customization
types we were inquired on from past experience:
• Custom protocol
Custom protocols are usually similar to our SOP, and in many cases, can be
accommodated by our service lab. However, it is mandatory for have the Lab Ops team
evaluate the protocol first. Any unforeseen need for additional equipment, consumable or
reagent may result in rejection after Stage 6, which should be avoided.
• Anti-coagulant
The choice of anti-coagulant does not have any impact on our SOPs. We have all common
blood tube types in stock, so there is no problem to adhere to customer's selection.
• Cryopreservation medium
It is generally considered that serum helps cells to recover from cryopreservation. Serum-
supplemented cryopreservation medium was a standard practice in research labs.
However, serum is a complex mixture obtained from living animals (human included), many
unidentified substances could interfere with downstream experiments, even after repetitive
cell washing. For scientists working on cell therapy R&D, serum-free cryopreservation
medium (i.e. CryoStor CS10 from Stemcell) is more desirable. We offer both options.
However, serum-supplemented medium is our default choice. If a serum-free
cryopreservation medium is needed, make sure the request is clearly communicated.

As a standard practice, we aliquot 1e6 PBMCs isolated from the whole blood into each

Miscellaneous Lab Requests
Pharmaceutical R&D varies greatly by nature. Service request from our customers are not
always limited to PBMC or serum/plasma isolation. Here is a short list of requests that we
are likely to come across:
• Additional cell types
We might have heard CAR T-cell therapy a lot, but the truth is R&D is exploring the
therapeutic potential on all known cell types, from the most common to the rarest. Since
we are operating in a heavily regulated space, we must have a SOP established before
we can offer a service. Therefore, we are currently limited to the most common sought-
after services, namely serum/plasma isolation, PBMC, T, B, NK cell isolation from whole
blood, as well as leukopak cryopreservation and PBMC isolation. Additional isolation types
are feasible, but need further review by the lab team and quality team. Some cell types are
rare in blood, such as CD34+ cells, γδ T cells, naïve T or B cells. Customer needing these
cell types tend to be more critical on delivered cell number and viability, so we would
discuss service feasibility cautiously, or reject the request to exonerate ourselves from the
liability.
• Large volume sample storage
We operate with limited space, both inside of our freezers, liquid nitrogen dewars, and in
the general space. Sometimes , an opportunity may involve storing large number of vials
(i.e. PBMC vials), tubes (i.e. swabs in tubes), cups (i.e. stool sample cup) or jars (i.e. urine
jars) for prolonged time. We need to understand the the volume (or space) requirement
before committing to the project. Contact the lab and feasibility team for an assessment.

Not feasible:
• Charcoal-striped serum/plasma
Charcoal is highly porous so it is sometimes used to deplete a wide range of peptides and
small molecules such as hormones and steroids by adsorption. Need for this sample type
is usually seen from scientists in early research, whom need to minimize the influence of
endogenous proteins (i.e. hormone) on their data. These customers typically do not need
large amount of samples, therefore, we do not offer this service in the lab.
• Complement-preserved serum/plasma
The complement system is a series of dissolved proteins that protect against a variety of
pathogens. It is found to be associated with cell lytic events that is commonly linked to
inflammation. Complement proteins will quickly degrade if a sample is not collected and
stored correctly. To preserve it, blood must be collected fresh and kept cold, and then
flash-frozen to prevent degradation of complement activity. Our in-home serum/plasma
isolation protocol is not validated for complement preservation. However, we may
thoroughly describe our procedure, which involves snap freezing serum/plasma on dry ice
right after isolation, therefore enabling our customers to make informed decisions.
• Cell culture
Cell culture is one of the most common blockers to many service requests. However, they
usually do not surface in the form of a direct ask for cell culture. So, we need to be able to
recognize service requests that have cell culture needs embedded. Generally, any cell-
based assay would require isolated cells incubated in a growth condition (to restore and

This type of demand is usually seen with start-ups, as well as companies with limited

By regulation, we are not allowed to process and store live animal samples (including
