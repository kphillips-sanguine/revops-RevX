Why Lab Testing
Lab testing of human samples involves analyzing biological specimens obtained from
individuals for various purposes, including diagnosis, monitoring, treatment selection, and
research.
At Sanguine, we support in-home collection and testing of most of the sample types
obtainable through non-invasive and minimally invasive collection methods.
Lab testing plays a crucial role in advancing pharmaceutical R&D, such as cell and gene
therapy, by providing valuable data and insights that inform the development, optimization,
and regulatory approval of these innovative treatments. Here are several reasons why lab
testing is often needed by our customers:
1. Safety Evaluation: Lab tests are essential for identifying potential harmful side effects
or adverse reactions, ensuring patient safety.
2. Efficacy Assessment: Lab testing helps researchers determine the effectiveness of
therapies by analyzing biomarkers, gene expression levels, or cellular functions.
3. Optimization of Protocols: Lab testing allows researchers to optimize protocols and
procedures for delivering therapies, determining optimal dosages, and refining
treatment protocols for better outcomes.
4. Understanding Mechanisms of Action: Lab testing aids in gaining insights into the
mechanisms underlying the therapies, enhancing understanding of how these therapies

5. Regulatory Compliance: Lab testing is necessary to meet regulatory requirements for

Viral Testing
Viral Testing:
Viruses are prevalent in human even the healthy population. Every person carries a diverse
array of viruses such as herpesviruses, adenoviruses, and respiratory viruses like influenza
and rhinoviruses as well as viruses that infect the bacteria in our bodies (bacteriophages).
Generally speaking, viruses are safety concerns in pharmaceutical research. In addition,
drug response is shown to be associated with infection history. Therefore, our customers
may need to selectively access donors with defined viral profiles.
Such screening can be performed by ordering a viral testing panel that covers
multiple common or relevant viruses such as a Respiratory Viral Panel (RVP).
Alternatively, individual tests can be ordered if only specific tests are required, such
as tests for Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV).
Viral testing can be performed on nucleic acid (DNA and RNA via whole blood) or serum
(antibody). Nucleic acid-based testing is highly sensitive and specific, allowing for the
detection of very small amounts of viral genetic material with high accuracy. It can identify
the presence of viruses even when they are present in low concentrations. Serum-based
testing may not be as sensitive as nucleic acid-based testing, particularly in the early
stages of infection when antibody levels may still be low. However, it can provide valuable
information about past or current viral infections and the immune response to those
infections.
It’s important to review what type of sample that is required for the test requested. If a test
requires Serum, we will need to do in-home processing which will impact pricing.

Rapid Viral Testing
Compared to viral testings that depend on a traditional laboratory, rapid viral testings are
designed to be fast, convenient, and often portable, allowing for rapid diagnosis and
decision-making in various healthcare settings.
Rapid viral testing includes three main methods:
1. Rapid Antigen Tests: Detect viral proteins on the virus surface, providing quick results
(15-30 minutes). Common for diagnosing respiratory viruses like influenza and
COVID-19.
2. Rapid Antibody Tests: Detect antibodies in blood, indicating past or current viral
infections. Results available within minutes, helpful for assessing immunity and
diagnosing certain viral diseases.v
3. Rapid Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAATs): Amplify and detect viral genetic
material (RNA or DNA) in various samples. Can deliver results in 15-60 minutes, useful
for diagnosing infections like influenza, RSV, and COVID-19.

Risks & Challenges
Although these tests excel in speed, it's important to note that rapid tests may have
limitations in terms of sensitivity and specificity compared to traditional laboratory-based
methods, and confirmatory testing may be necessary in some cases.
This is because rapid tests are usually (with exceptions) based on immunological reactions
that detect antigens in blood.
• False negative results may be reported because the level of antigen is below the

CLIA
CLIA
The Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments of 1988 (CLIA) regulations include
federal standards applicable to all U.S. facilities or sites that test human specimens
for health assessment or to diagnose, prevent, or treat disease. They are designed to
ensure the accuracy, reliability, and quality of laboratory testing performed on human
specimens for the purpose of diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of disease.
CLIA regulations cover a wide range of aspects related to laboratory operations, including
personnel qualifications, quality control procedures, proficiency testing, recordkeeping, and
inspection requirements. Compliance with CLIA standards is essential for ensuring the
accuracy and reliability of laboratory testing results and for maintaining patient safety.
CLIA is important for several reasons:
1. Ensuring Accuracy and Reliability: CLIA standards guarantee that clinical lab tests
provide accurate and reliable results, vital for proper patient care and monitoring.
2. Protecting Patient Safety: Stringent quality control measures mandated by CLIA
safeguard patients from misdiagnosis or inappropriate treatment, ensuring their safety.
3. Promoting Public Health: CLIA's high-quality standards in lab testing aid disease
surveillance, outbreak detection, and overall public health monitoring.
4. Standardizing Laboratory Practices: CLIA ensures consistent and uniform testing
procedures across laboratories, regardless of size or location, enhancing the quality of
care patients receive.

CLIA Tests
CLIA tests
The most popular CLIA tests we use are the following:
• CBC (Complete Blood Count)
This is a common blood test that provides important information about the types and
numbers of cells in your blood. It typically includes measurements of three main types of
cells: red blood cells (RBCs), White blood cells (WBCs) and Platelets. In addition to
counting these cells, a CBC test may also measure other parameters such as hemoglobin
concentration, hematocrit, and mean corpuscular volume (MCV), which can provide further
insights into a donor’s health.
As CBC is a standardized test that provides basal information on the health condition of a
donor, it may be requested by our customers interested in any morbidity.
• CRP
The CRP test, or C-reactive protein test, is a blood test that measures the level of C-
reactive protein in your blood. C-reactive protein (CRP) is a substance produced by the
liver in response to inflammation in the body. The CRP test is often used as a marker of
inflammation, as elevated levels of CRP can indicate the presence of inflammation in the
body.

• dsDNA
At Sanguine, we categorize any test that needs to be performed on DNA and RNA as
“dsDNA”. These tests generally involve isolating nucleic acids (DNA and/or RNA) from
whole blood collected in PAXgene tubes. The nucleic acid is subsequently used in genetic
analysis that are typically quantitative for level of expression determination, or qualitative
for presence/absence determination. To assess the feasibility and requirement, we need
our customers to provide the name of genes of interest or gene panel.
• Urinalysis
Urinalysis is a diagnostic test that involves examining the physical, chemical, and
microscopic properties of urine. It is commonly used to assess the health of the urinary
system and to detect various medical conditions affecting the kidneys, bladder, or other
parts of the urinary tract, as well as systemic diseases that may manifest in changes to
urine composition.
In pharmaceutical R&D, urinalysis can be used to screen for certain medical conditions,
monitor the progression of diseases affecting the urinary system, or to assess the
effectiveness of a treatment.

Leukopak Viral Testing
Leukopak Viral Testing
Compared to a whole blood draw, a leukopak is advantageous in studies that require large
quantities of PBMCs from the same donor, usually in the billions. However, unlike cells
grown in a laboratory setting, human samples may carry unknown risks (i.e. virus and other
pathogens) that pose threat to the safety of researchers as well as concerns of
contamination to other valuable biological samples in the same lab.
Viral testing is even more critical in GMP settings where engineered cells from a leukopak
would enter the production phase of cell therapy, as the final product would be eventually
transfused to a patient or many patients.
As such, we enforce viral and infectious disease testing (IDT) on every leukopak we
produce. Our SOP involves scheduling a screening visit for each donor prior to
leukapheresis. During this visit, viral testing as well as other IDTs are performed by drawing
a small volume of their whole blood which is subsequently sent to LabCorp for read-out.
These viral screens are accurate for 3 months.
For these, we complete the following STANDARD testing:

• HBsAg
