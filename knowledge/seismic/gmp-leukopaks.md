Overview
GMP Leukopak Definition
Collections of white blood cells (leukopaks) obtained through a process called leukapheresis,
conducted under Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) conditions.
GMP leukopaks are collected under specific conditions that adhere to standards set by the
FDA.
• Guidelines present in the Code of Federal Regulations 21 Part 1271 (21 CFR 1271) must
be followed for the collections. Regulations include:
◦Specific donor testing panel
◦Health history questionnaire (HHQ)
◦CoA
◦Specific quality documentation must be performed to qualify a product for GMP.
GMP Leukopaks can be after further manufacturing for clinical or therapeutic usage in human
subjects
Leukopak Overview
• Leukopaks contain a high concentration of peripheral blood mononuclear cells
(PBMCs) – which consists of T cells, B cells, NK cells, and monocytes. These products
can contain (10 Billion) cells per donor
Leukopak Collections:

---

<!-- Page 3 -->
• Leukopaks are collected at an apheresis collection site, and must be done under the
supervision of a registered nurse
• The leukapheresis procedure takes ~3-4 hours for each collection, and is performed on
a Spectra Optia collection device
• During leukapheresis, the Optia device acts as a giant "centrifuge," removing white
blood cells from the donor's whole blood and returning the other blood components
back to the donor (red blood cells, plasma, platelets, etc.)

Allogenic Cell Types
Allogeneic cell therapy
Allogeneic cell therapy is a type of cell therapy that uses cells from a donor to treat other
patients. The donor can be related or unrelated to the patient, but the cells must be from the
same species.
The cells can be unmodified or modified. Allogeneic cell therapy can be used to treat a variety
of conditions, including blood cancers, immune system disorders, and aplastic anemia.
Allogenic Cell Therapy Companies need GMP grade starting materials (leukopaks) for further
manufacturing and eventual re-infusion into donors
• Why is pharma investing more in Allogeneic Cell Therapy?

---

<!-- Page 9 -->
• Scalability: One of the biggest advantages of allogeneic therapy is that cells from a
single donor can be used to treat multiple patients, making it more scalable and
potentially more cost-effective compared to autologous therapies.
• On-Demand Availability: Unlike autologous therapies, where cells must be harvested
from each patient and processed, allogeneic therapies can be stored and ready for
use, which is particularly important in urgent medical situations.
• Consistency: Using cells from healthy donors reduces variability, as the quality and
characteristics of donor cells can be tightly controlled and standardized for clinical use.
• The cells used in allogeneic therapy must be manufactured in facilities that
comply with Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) to ensure product quality,
consistency, and safety.

Sales Process
GMP Leukopak Projects are segmented into different phases
1. Donor Screening & Identification (RUO)
a. 5 - 100+ unique donors
b. 2-12 months
c. GMP Leukopak projects often begin with a client wanting to screen a population of
healthy donors and receive RUO leukopaks→ anywhere from 5-100+ donors, and
this phase can take anywhere from 2-12 months.
Why Screen?
• Researchers screen potential donors to find those whose cells are most
compatible or optimal for specific allogeneic therapies. Key characteristics
assessed during screening include:
• HLA Typing: Researchers perform human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing to
ensure that the donor’s immune cells have a high likelihood of compatibility
with a broad range of recipients.
• Cell Functionality Tests: Researchers evaluate the viability, quantity,
functionality, and immune characteristics of cells (e.g., T-cells, NK cells, or
other white blood cells) in the leukopaks to ensure that they will perform as
needed in therapies.

---

<!-- Page 13 -->
• Biomarker Analysis: Specific biomarkers or genetic traits of donors are
assessed using leukopaks to ensure that they meet criteria for use in
allogeneic therapies.
2. Donor Recalls for Process Development, Manufacturing, and Clinical Applications
(GMP)
a. Reserved donors who may only participate in this project
b. Collections planned every 6 weeks
c. Months to years
d. After a client has identified a cohort of donors that work well for their specific
needs, the client will proceed with GMP grade collections.
• Clients will often require multiple recollections (every six weeks) over the
course of several months or even years in order to support the need for
multiple manufacturing runs for each donor’s cells
• Each manufacturing run is a distinct batch or cycle of production, where raw
biological materials (usually cells) undergo a series of steps to meet clinical or
commercial needs

therapeutic doses of allogeneic cells. These cells are collected (now by

Pre-Clinical Researchers
• Title Examples: Research Associate, Cell Therapy Scientist, VP of Cell Therapy
Research
• Role: Pre-clinical researchers are responsible for the initial phases of developing a cell
therapy product. They conduct experiments to study the biology of the therapeutic
cells, test their effectiveness in models (e.g., in vitro cell cultures and animal studies),
and assess safety and efficacy before moving into clinical trials.
• Usage of GMP Leukopaks: Leukopaks may be used for cell isolation,
differentiation, and expansion in laboratory experiments to optimize cell therapies

---

before they move into clinical-grade manufacturing.
These researchers will be working with RUO grade leukopaks in the GMP sales process
Process Development Scientists at Cell Therapy Companies
• Title Examples: Process Development Scientist, Senior Process Engineer – Cell
Therapy, Bioprocess Development Engineer, Associate Scientist – Process
Development
• Role: Process development scientists work to scale up and optimize the cell therapy
manufacturing process. They develop and refine the procedures needed to produce
large quantities of therapeutic cells under GMP conditions. This includes optimizing
cell expansion, cell washing, storage, and delivery processes to ensure product
consistency, quality, and reproducibility
• Usage of GMP Leukopaks: Process development scientists use leukopaks to develop
and optimize manufacturing methods, ensuring that the cells derived from leukopaks
can be expanded and processed consistently under GMP standards.
These researchers might be working with RUO or GMP grade leukopaks in the GMP sales
process

Screening & Collection Visits
Screening & Collection Visits
Supply of GMP leukopaks is dependent on the number of available healthy RUO
donors in Sanguine’s Los Angeles network. Sanguine has plenty of donor already
recruited who have successfully donated RUO leukopaks in the past (100+ donors)
• Efforts will be made for nursing staff to identify “GMP Ready” donors– reliable
individuals who have proven to be in good health, with good venous access,
and no track record of rescheduling/no-shows for collections
• These GMP Ready donors are being identified pre-launch and will continue to
be identified as the product offering matures.
Leukopak collections occur in a two visit approach for both disease state and healthy
collections.
1. Screening Visit for Apheresis
• First, donors are recruited remotely by Sanguine research coordinators and consented
remotely. If needed, medical records are pulled. This mirrors our translational process
exactly
• Next, donors are sent to either the LA site or a partner apheresis site for a screening

---

<!-- Page 26 -->
visit. During this visit, a health history questionnaire is filled out (HHQ), the donor
undergoes a physical assessment by the nurse, and blood tubes are drawn for CBC
and Infectious disease testing.
• The screening visit usually takes about 1 hour and requires the donor to travel to the
site
2. Collection visit
• Once the donor's CBC and IDT results are received from our partner lab (results
received usually ~1 week post screening visit), Labcorp, then the donor is scheduled
for a collection visit.
• The collection visit is scheduled anywhere from 1 week–30 days after the screening
visit occurs, and is dependent on both client and donor availability
• The collection visit is where the Leukopak product is collected in a process that takes
about 3-4 hours
• After the Leukopak is collected, it is packaged in a NanoCool shipping container,
validated for 2-8 degrees Celsius, and delivered directly to the client or shipped to
the Sanguine lab for cryopreservation/cell isolation
• TNC counts are generated on the same day of collection
• CoAs are generated within 24 hours of collection
• The standard courier used for all leukopak collections is Airspace
