Overview of Clinical Trials
Clinical trials are research studies conducted to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of
new medical treatments, interventions, or procedures in humans. These trials are essential
steps in the process of bringing new drugs, devices, or treatments to market and improving
medical care. They typically follow a series of phases:
1. Phase 0: Phase 0 trials are exploratory studies that involve very limited human
exposure to the drug or treatment, often with just a few participants. These trials aim to
gather preliminary data on how the drug behaves in the human body, such as its
pharmacokinetics (how the body absorbs, distributes, metabolizes, and excretes the
drug).
2. Phase I: Phase I trials involve a small group of healthy volunteers (and sometimes
patients) and focus primarily on evaluating the safety and dosage of the treatment.
Researchers aim to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and any side effects
or adverse reactions associated with the treatment.
3. Phase II: Phase II trials expand the study to a larger group of patients (usually several
hundred) who have the condition or disease that the treatment is intended to address.
These trials assess both the safety and efficacy of the treatment, typically over a period
of several months to a year. Researchers aim to gather more data on the treatment's
effectiveness and further evaluate its safety profile.
4. Phase III: Phase III trials are large-scale studies that involve hundreds to thousands of
patients. These trials aim to confirm and further evaluate the treatment's effectiveness,
monitor side effects, and compare it to standard or existing treatments (if applicable).
Phase III trials provide the most robust evidence of a treatment's benefits and risks
and are crucial for regulatory approval by agencies such as the FDA (Food and Drug
Administration) in the United States.

---

5. Phase IV: Phase IV trials, also known as post-marketing studies, occur after a
treatment has been approved and is available to the general public. These trials
continue to monitor the treatment's safety and effectiveness in larger, more diverse
patient populations over an extended period. Phase IV trials help identify rare or long-
term side effects and gather additional real-world data on the treatment's performance.
Throughout all phases of clinical trials, participants are closely monitored by researchers
and healthcare professionals to ensure their safety and well-being. Informed consent is
obtained from all participants, and ethical guidelines are followed to protect their rights and
privacy. The results of clinical trials contribute to advancing medical knowledge, informing
clinical practice, and ultimately improving patient care

◦Concern: poaching of patients

Assumptions will look like the following:
• Sanguine will operate as a recruitment vendor for the client, in so that Sanguine will
prescreen donors from Sanguine’s database and prequalify them based on specified
inclusion/exclusion criteria.
• Sanguine cannot guarantee that all values for inclusion/exclusion selected by the client
will be available in the subject's medical record. If a value is missing from the medical
record:
◦Sanguine will attempt to obtain an updated medical record and will not exclude
the patient. Sanguine will perform a maximum of 2 medical record pulls per
patient.
◦If Sanguine finds that a significant value(s) is/are missing and is unable to obtain
this value from medical records, Sanguine will consult with CLIENT regarding
eligibility.
• After prescreening and prequalifying the subject, the subject will sign a HIPAA
authorization form so Sanguine can release the subject's information to the potential
study site. Sanguine will then perform a “warm handoff” defined as Sanguine sharing
the name, contact information, and redacted medical record summaries via Box to the
relevant clinical trial site.
• It is the client's responsibility to inform Sanguine of any study site changes, including
closures and/or activations. Study sites are responsible for follow up and enrollment of
prequalified patients. Study sites will be responsible for updating the study tracker to
inform Sanguine of any enrollments. Sanguine will be notified monthly by the clinical
sites and/or CLIENT about successful enrollments into the clinical trial.

Why do clients want mobile services?
• Decreases burden for participation
• Increases patient retention
• Build patient centricity
• Removes “brick and mortar restraints”
