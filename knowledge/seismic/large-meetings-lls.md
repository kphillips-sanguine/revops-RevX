Overview
Scoring a meeting with a researcher is great, but what happens if you get a meeting with
10? 20? 50? How do you plan accordingly? Adjust your presentation to be more effective?
We've been seeing a growing interest in Lunch and Learns to educate more researchers on
our services, which result in more opportunities.
In this training, we will discuss how to land and successfully execute upon large meetings
and Lunch & Learn presentations.

Scheduling
Agenda
When scheduling a large meeting or lunch & learn, you should schedule at least a one hour
meeting. It is good to coordinate with your host if you should have a longer time frame.
Depending on your champion and the account, you may want to turn a lunch & learn
request into a several smaller meetings by asking questions like:
• What would you like to accomplish during the lunch & learn?
• Who all do think would be attending?
• We can do a lunch & learn with everyone, we can also do smaller meetings based on
therapeutic areas so we can customize the presentation. Would you be open to that?
After you agree upon the group and the time you have, make an agenda and a synopsis of
what the presentation will be about. For example:
"Scott O'Donnell from Sanguine Biosciences will be presenting on their capabilities to
obtain and process minimally invasive biospecimens and leukopaks from both diseased
and healthy donors to accelerate Research & Development.
Scott has been working for Sanguine for over 4 years and has worked on over 400
translational and clinical studies, across various indications. He has been working with
Merck for over 3 years and looks forward to continuing to grow the Merck and Sanguine
partnership.

Food
If you plan to offer lunch, it is important to see if you are allowed to bring lunch/food/
snacks. Many large pharma companies have policies about what they can and cannot
accept. If they say you can supply:
• Ask if they have a suggestion for a local place
◦Usually sandwich places are a safe bet
• Confirm how many people will be attending so you can order appropriately
◦If it's a smaller L&L, you can take individual orders when it gets closer
• Confirm if there are any allergies / dietary restrictions you should be aware of

Preparing
Slide deck
1. Prepare a Sanguine/Company Relationship slide. There is a template saved in
workspace you can add and then edit and adjust to your account. Things that are important
to add:
• How long you've worked together (if over 2+ years)
• How many studies you've worked on
• What indications you've worked on
• Contracting status with that account (CDA, MSA, MTA, Scientist.com/
ScienceExchange Provider)
• Relevant Case Studies / Feedback
2. If they have a robust pipeline, on the slide that highlights all of our indications, BOLD the
indications in their pipeline so it's easy to see that overlap.
• Make sure you thoroughly review what they are working on, previous requests you've
received, which departments you've worked with / haven't worked with.
3. Plan out relevant questions on specific slides, especially ones you've received from that
account.
• This will encourage people to ask more questions
• It will also give some answers in a natural flow that are important to the client
• Here are some examples:

Preparing
If you are given a list of people who will be attending, do your due diligence and look them
up on LinkedIn and get an understanding of who you are presenting to:
• Therapeutic area
• Personas
• Groups within the organization (same group, different groups)
• Existing contacts vs new leads
• Historical Sanguine usage (previously worked at one of our active clients)

Presenting
Introduction
Best Practice: Have someone from their company introduce you and share why they
wanted to a lunch and learn. This gives you instant credibility and gets people’s
guards down because they are endorsing you.
Still set your Ground Rules:
Thank you all for coming today! As ABC mentioned, I’m (name) and I’m (role) here at
Sanguine. Over the next hour we’re going to cover A, B, and C. Please feel free to interrupt
me at anytime for questions but I will leave some time for Q&A at the end, and I'll be
sticking around after this presentation if you have a specific need or opportunity that you’d
like to discuss.
Customizing
As mentioned previously in the preparing section, it is very important you know who you
are presenting to. You can use this information to customize your presentation. For
example, if you are presenting to a group heavily focused in Autoimmune with SLE and UC
in their pipeline. Here are some ways you can adjust your talk tracks:
• When you speak about our community: "We have over 25,000 autoimmune patients
within our community and this number is consistently growing."
• When you highlight the indications: Point our SLE and UC, highlight how many patients
we have in each of those indications and the delivered to plan success rates

• When you speak about Patient Data: Give real examples of something relevant to that
patient population that helps prove your credibility:
◦If you were looking at an SLE patient, you could see if they are on
Hydroxychloroquine or prednisone/steroids
◦We are gathering from all of their providers, not just their main rheumatologist -
getting insight from their ophthalmologist, dermatologist etc.
Differentiating Sanguine
It is very important in these meetings to dive deep. Typically we go through our
presentations fairly quickly, but you have more time in a L&L and you want to be very
thorough. With large groups, you never know what is going to jump out to someone. This is
why it's so important to ensure you're hitting on all of Sanguine's value adds and explaining
our model in a digestible manner.
Highlight our Direct-to-Patient Model and why it's important. We usually have a more
conversational dialogue in 1:1 meetings where we can ask about the struggles they've had
with sample collections or their other providers, but in large meetings people aren't willing
to speak up and you don't want to have crickets when you ask a question. During this
explanation, lean on the talk track and pull out 2-3 examples of challenges you've seen in
the market (BONUS if those challenges came from some of the employees at that account
so you know they will resonate) and why Sanguine helps solve those challenges (bridging
that gap!)
Value Props
• Direct-to-Patient Model
• Verified Patients
• Standardized Patient Data

Q&A
• In the beginning, ask people to ask questions throughout. For example:
◦"I know we have quite a few people here today. And sometimes when I’m doing
larger meetings people are hesitant to talk or ask questions, but please, do not
hesitate. The more information we can share, the better."
• Leave time at the end for Q&A
• Invite people to stay and discuss any upcoming sample needs or projects afterwards
("I'll be sticking around for the next half hour or so if anyone wants to meet and discuss
any current or future projects")
• Always repeat the question back in case people cannot hear the person asking (very
important if you have a combination of Zoom and In-Person)

Post Meeting
You did it! Successful presentation. Now what?
1. Thank your host!
2. Create a Digital Sales Room with all relevant information that you shared:
a. Slide Deck
b. Overview of Capabilities
c. Community
3. Send it out to all of the attendees
4. Connect with them all on LinkedIn with a personalized note, thanking them
5. Put them all into a cadence to nurture and follow up consistently
6. Schedule some time to ask for feedback from your host - how did it go? what jumped
out to people? was it helpful? should we do this with another group? etc.
7. Try and schedule another :) or at least some follow up meetings.

Virtual Meetings
The more people you have on a call, the easier it is for people to become passive
listeners because:
• They’re not clear about the purpose of the meeting.
• They don’t want to derail or dominate the conversation.
• They’re hesitant to share their fears, concerns, or desires with the larger group.
• The “S” and “C” feel uncomfortable and take a backside to the more talkative “D” and
“I.”
• It’s easier to get sidetracked by distractions.
With that said you want to follow some best practices to create a safe space for
engagement.
Tips
• Send email to all the attendees asking they’re biggest priority. For example, “In
preparation for our meeting on ______________, please send me the top few things
you’d like to discuss or questions you’d like answered so we can maximize our

time together.”
• Start your meetings five minutes early so you have time for small talk.
• If they don’t send their top 2-3 things in advance, you can ask as they’re coming onto
the call. You can also use the chat feature to capture this information.
• Set ground rules and let them know if it’s going to be a conversation, not a
presentation.
• Tell them what you don’t want to happen. For example, “I know we have quite a few
people on the call today. And sometimes when I’m doing larger meetings people
are hesitant to talk. Please, do not hesitate. The more information we get from
people in different roles, the better.
• Invite people into the conversation by calling on specific people for input. For example,
“Jared I know you’re in a unique role so your needs might be different. Would you
mind shared what data is most important to you?” (reference an old conversation
or something you know is relevant to them)
• When you ask a question, give people time to respond. Count to five in your head
before moving on, especially if you have a Q&A section. The silence will feel awkward
the first few times, but it’s highly effective.
• If someone jumps on the call late, acknowledge them, let them know what you’re
