Introduction
Drug discovery is a complex process that involves identifying and developing new
medications to treat medical conditions. Scientists focus on understanding diseases and
identifying potential drug targets. They use biospecimens and advanced technologies like
genomics and high-throughput screening to unravel disease mechanisms and identify
potential drug candidates. Despite challenges, the integration of advanced research tools
has significantly improved the efficiency and success rate of drug discovery efforts.

Phases of drug discovery
Drug discovery is a complex, multi-step process typically divided into several key phases:
1. Target Identification and Validation: Researchers identify biological molecules
(targets) associated with a disease and validate their role in disease mechanisms.
2. Hit Discovery: Screening large compound libraries to identify "hits"—molecules that
show potential activity against the target. This can involve high-throughput screening,
virtual screening, or other methods.
3. Hit to Lead Optimization: Hits are further characterized and optimized to improve
their efficacy, selectivity, and pharmacokinetic properties. This often involves chemical
modifications and testing.
4. Lead Optimization: The best candidates (leads) are refined to enhance their
properties, such as potency, stability, and safety. This phase involves iterative rounds
of testing and optimization.
5. Preclinical Development: Promising candidates undergo rigorous testing in vitro (in
lab settings) and in vivo (in animal models) to assess their safety, efficacy,

---

<!-- Page 4 -->
pharmacokinetics, and toxicity.
6. Clinical Development: If preclinical studies are successful, the drug enters clinical
trials, which are typically conducted in three phases:
• Phase I: Tests safety and dosage in a small group of healthy volunteers.
• Phase II: Assesses efficacy and side effects in a larger group of patients.
• Phase III: Conducted on large populations to confirm efficacy, monitor side
effects, and compare with standard treatments.
7. Regulatory Review: After successful clinical trials, the drug is submitted for regulatory
approval (e.g., to the FDA). This involves a thorough review of all data.
8. Post-Marketing Surveillance: Once approved, the drug is monitored for long-term
effects and safety in the general population, often referred to as Phase IV studies.
Each phase is critical for ensuring that a drug is safe and effective before it reaches the
market.

Early Stage Drug Discovery
Early Stage discovery is where we fit in, particularly from Target Identification through Pre-
Clinical.
Early stage drug discovery often involves the utilization of a wide variety of
biospecimens, such as blood, tissue samples, and genetic material, to comprehend
the intricate molecular and cellular mechanisms involved in the disease.
These biospecimens are crucial in unraveling the biological pathways and potential targets
for the development of therapeutic interventions. Cutting-edge technologies like genomics,
proteomics, and metabolomics are employed to analyze these biospecimens, providing
deep insights into the disease's pathology and identifying potential drug targets.
Genomics
Genomics is the branch of molecular biology that focuses on the study of genomes, which
are the complete set of DNA in an organism, including all of its genes.
• Gene sequencing
• Gene Identification
• Comparative Genomics: Comparing genomes from different species to identify
similarities and differences
• Functional Genomics: Investigating the function of genes and their products (proteins),
often using techniques like RNA sequencing or knockout studies to understand gene
roles

• Epigenomics: Studying the epigenetic modifications that affect gene expression without
altering the DNA sequence itself, such as DNA methylation and histone modification.
• Metagenomics: Analyzing genetic material obtained directly from environmental

need for culturing.
*Samples with a high source of DNA and RNA are most relevant including blood, saliva, urine,

Biomarker Research
Biomarker
Biological Markers (biomarkers) - used for various purposes, such as diagnosing diseases,
predicting disease progression, monitoring treatment responses, or identifying individuals at
risk for certain conditions
A biological target (usually a protein such as an enzyme, receptor, or ion channel) must be
identified and validated. This target should be associated with the disease mechanism.
Target validation ensures that modulating the target will have a therapeutic effect on the
disease.
The goal of biomarker research is to find a "hit discovery." A hit is when compounds show
initial signs of activity against a specific biological target.

Preclinical Development
Preclinical Development
Preclinical development is a stage in the drug development process that occurs before
clinical trials (testing in humans) and after the early stages of drug discovery, such as hit
discovery and lead optimization.
The primary goal of preclinical development is to evaluate the safety, efficacy,
pharmacokinetics, and pharmacodynamics of potential drug candidates in non-human
models (typically cell cultures and animal studies).
• Timeframe: Preclinical development usually takes 1-3 years, depending on the
complexity of the studies and the results.
• Costs: Preclinical studies are expensive, accounting for a significant portion of early
drug development costs, due to the complexity of safety, efficacy, and regulatory
requirements.

• Pharmacokinetics (PK) - PK studies determine how a drug moves through the body

◦These studies provide information on the half-life (how long the drug stays in the

◦**sometimes researchers will ask what the half life is to understand the wash out

• Pharmacodynamics (PD) - assess the biological effects of the drug on the body and
the relationship between the drug concentration and its therapeutic or toxic effects.
• Efficacy testing (in-vivo) - in animal models (often rodents) is designed to determine if
the drug has the desired therapeutic effect in disease-relevant settings.
• Safety pharmacology focuses on assessing the effects of the drug on vital organ
systems such as the cardiovascular, respiratory, and central nervous systems.
• Formulation Development - develop a drug formulation that can be safely and
effectively administered to humans.
These phases - Toxicology, PD and PK are mainly focused in animal model blood
vs humans, but in clinical trial phases they will need to evaluate these samples in
human participants.

Regulatory Submissions
Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) Compliance

---

<!-- Page 14 -->
Preclinical studies, especially toxicology studies, must be conducted under Good Laboratory
Practice (GLP) regulations, ensuring the quality and integrity of the data.
IND
• All the data generated during preclinical development is compiled into an Investigational
New Drug (IND) application or Clinical Trial Application (CTA) for regulatory
submission to agencies such as the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or the
European Medicines Agency (EMA).
• The IND application contains detailed information about the drug's pharmacology,
toxicology, manufacturing, and planned clinical protocols.
• Approval of the IND by regulatory authorities is required before the drug can proceed to
clinical trials (testing in humans).
After receiving IND approval, biological samples continue to play a vital role in the
clinical development of the drug. These samples are necessary for assessing the
drug’s safety, efficacy, and mechanism of action during clinical trials, developing

Therefore, collecting and analyzing biological samples remains a critical part of drug

Challenges in Drug Discovery
Despite the incredible advancements in technology, the process of drug discovery remains
challenging due to the complex nature of diseases and the demanding regulatory
requirements. However, the integration of advanced research tools and methodologies has
significantly improved the efficiency and success rate of drug discovery efforts.

Conclusion
In conclusion, early stage drug discovery is a critical phase in the development of new
medications, and scientists in this domain are focused on leveraging advanced
technologies and biospecimens to identify potential drug candidates for a wide array of
diseases. The utilization of biospecimens, in conjunction with cutting-edge technologies,
plays a pivotal role in unraveling disease mechanisms and ultimately facilitates the
development of novel therapeutic interventions for various medical conditions.
