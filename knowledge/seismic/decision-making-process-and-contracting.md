Decision Making Process
Decision Making
The decision making process is not just one item you check off in a deal, it is a series of
steps you make throughout the course of the opportunity to help drive it to a close. There
are several elements that go into the process including:
• Driving reason they are potentially choosing Sanguine. What pain are we trying to
solve?
• Addressing Concerns/Objections - what concerns do they have with moving forward
with Sanguine? How have we addressed them
• Identifying all relevant parties
• Contracting
• Negotiation
And the burning question:
how long does that process typically take?"

Contracting
Contracting
If contracting is not brought up early in the conversation, it may cause bottlenecks down
the road. Existing contracts should always be evaluated in the very beginning:
• Do we have a CDA (confidentiality disclosure agreement) or NDA (non-disclosure
agreement) in place?
◦This allows the client to speak freely early on in the opportunity
• Do we have an MSA (Master Services Agreement) in place?
◦Is it expired?
◦If we have a MSA already in place, all proposals must go into a SOW (Statement of
work) also called Work Order (WO)
• Are you using scientist.com/ScienceExchange?
◦If yes, we are a vendor on these platforms.
◦No further MSA is needed
New Clients
It's very important to ask early on if there are any new vendor requirements in order to work
together in the future. Sometimes there are:
• Vendor Qualification Questionnaires or Assessments
• Audit Requirements
Sometimes these can take a long time, or be a quick process. Whenever you ask about a

Timelines for contracts
Existing Clients
It's always good to double check the decision making process, even with existing clients.
"Typically with XYZ account, they use the Scientist.com platform. Is that what your team
uses as well?"
"The last time we worked together, the SOW approval with your legal team took a couple
weeks. Do you anticipate it taking the same amount of time for this project"
Referencing old projects and how you've worked with that account goes a long way with
clients for building credibility, but then also understanding their decision making process to
give accurate forecasts.
New Clients
It's good to ask general questions on the decision making process that you can then get
more granular with. For example:
"Since we haven't worked with you in the past, if you did want to move forward with this
project, what does it look like to get everything finalized on your side?"
Timing
"If a client says, oh it's a pretty quick process"
How would you respond?
"That's great. Quick can mean different things for different clients - is it typically like 2 days
or like 2 weeks" You always want to take that process one step further.

Timelines for samples
It's end of quarter. You want to close the deal. The client doesn't need these samples until
the end of the year. What is the rush to close?
If you don't have a reason that BENEFITS THEM, you do not have enough urgency to close
that deal. Rather, if you know they need Flu Vaccine Studies and Flu season starts in
September and they need a custom protocol, they have a reason to close that deal by early
August if they want to get the right samples.
If they DO need these samples by a certain time, always use the work backwards
approach. If they need these samples in 4 months and our timeline says 3 months, this
adds urgency for them to close the deal prior to the end of the month to meet THEIR
timeline.

Approval workflows
Approval Workflows
Every company has different approval workflows for their deals. It's good to ask, what does
your process look like to get a project like this approved?
Even during discovery or study design meetings, you can always give a hypothetical.
Here's an example:
"I'll work on getting the proposal ready for you. If everything looks good and aligns with
that ballpark estimate and you decide you want to move forward, what would the next
steps look like to do so on your side?"
Thresholds
There are certain tiers or thresholds that contacts can approve. For example, a Director
may be able to approve up to $50,000. Anything above that will have to go up to the VP
level, anything above $200,000 may have to go up to c-suite approval. If it has to go up
higher than your contacts level, it may make sense to ask to join the meeting with the
approver to be able to answer any questions (and ensure they see the value!)
It's good to understand where your deal falls on their threshold level, and if there are other
people that need to be brought into the deal. You should keep notes of these thresholds for
future deals.
If a client needs to remain under a threshold for quick approval, it may make sense to split
up the contracts if needed. In the past for a same-day order, the client consistently did
smaller contracts so they didn't have to go for higher-up approvals. Asking if this would be
a good option can be a good solution.

Legal Involvement
Oftentimes legal involvement can be a bottleneck, whether that's redlining the MSA or
approving the SOW.
If we are having a lot of redlines and back and forth with their legal team, it is a good idea
to have our contracts team suggest a meeting to hash out any questions or concerns to
speed up the process.

Scenarios
Scenario 1:
The client is so excited to work with Sanguine. The proposal looks great. The pricing
seemed to be what they were expecting. They're sending it up to their manager and it'll
hopefully get approved soon since they want to kick off right away. But then, they decide to
go with another vendor. What went wrong? Why did we lose?
This happens time and time again, and one of the reasons is that WE don't challenge the
client. When we think back on our DISC training, there are certain personas who are "Yes"
people. They'd rather say everything looks great, and then ghost you when they decide to
go a different direction because they don't want to say no.
How do we prevent that? We can put them on the spot.
"Great, I'm so glad this is inline with what you were expecting. When you bring this over to
your management, do you foresee anything stopping them from moving forward?"
From here, you may actually get some of the concerns that you can address, or see any red
flags that may arise.
Scenario 2:
Client is interested in a really large project. They came to Sanguine because they are aware
of our services and really want the patient data we can provide. When you ask when they
plan to make their decision to move forward or not, they say they are still looking at a
couple other vendors. What do you say?
"I completely understand for a project of this size you're looking at multiple vendors. For
